# CA — Security Deposit Law
## Last Verified: 2026-06-12
## Source: https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=CIV&sectionNum=1950.5
## Next Review Due: 2027-06-12
## Status: active

## 1. STATUTE REFERENCE
- Primary: California Civil Code § 1950.5
- Related: § 1946 (notice of termination), § 1946.1 (60-day notice), § 1950.6 (application screening fee)

---

## 2. DEPOSIT LIMITS
- Maximum allowed: 2 months' rent (unfurnished); 3 months' rent (furnished) — § 1950.5(c)
- Notes: Applies to residential tenancies. Amount must be stated in the lease or rental agreement.

---

## 3. HOLDING REQUIREMENTS
- Must be held in separate account: No statutory requirement — § 1950.5 does not require segregation
- Interest bearing required: No — not required by § 1950.5 (local rent control ordinances may vary)
- Tenant must be notified of account: No — not required by § 1950.5
- Notes: Landlord may commingle deposit with other funds unless a local rent control ordinance requires otherwise

---

## 4. MOVE-OUT NOTICE REQUIREMENTS
- Written notice required: Yes — for month-to-month tenancies, written notice is required per Civil Code § 1946
- Notice period: Tenant must provide at least 30 days' written notice; landlord must provide 30 or 60 days depending on tenancy length — § 1946, § 1946.1
- Notice period source: California Civil Code § 1946 (not § 1950.5)
- Method required: Written — § 1946
- Notes: Failure to provide required notice may give landlord grounds to deduct lost rent. Lease parser must extract this clause specifically.

---

## 5. RETURN DEADLINE
- Days to return: 21 calendar days — § 1950.5(a), (b)
- Deadline trigger: Date tenant vacates premises. For tenancies requiring notice under § 1946, landlord need not comply earlier than the end of the notice period, but must still comply no later than 21 days after the tenant has vacated.
- Forwarding address required: Not specified in statute — landlord must deliver to the tenant's last known address
- Effect of no forwarding address: Not specified in statute — landlord must send the itemization and any refund to the tenant's last known address by personal delivery or first-class mail, postage prepaid

---

## 6. ITEMIZATION REQUIREMENTS
- Written itemization required: Yes — § 1950.5(a), (b)
- Must accompany return: Yes — within the same 21-day deadline
- Required contents:
  - Description of each deduction: Yes — "basis for... the disposition of the security"
  - Dollar amount per deduction: Yes — "amount of... the disposition of the security"
  - Receipts required: Yes — for repairs, cleaning, or materials/supplies, landlord must include copies of invoices or receipts; if work was performed by the landlord or employee, the statement must describe the work performed, time spent, and hourly rate — § 1950.5(f)
- Delivery method: Personal delivery or first-class mail, postage prepaid, to the tenant's last known address — § 1950.5(a), (b)

---

## 7. ALLOWABLE DEDUCTIONS
- Unpaid rent: Yes — § 1950.5(b)(1)
- Damage beyond normal wear and tear: Yes — § 1950.5(b)(2)
- Cleaning if specified in lease: Yes — limited to cleaning necessary to return the unit to the same level of cleanliness it was in at the inception of the tenancy — § 1950.5(b)(3). Non-refundable cleaning fees and blanket cleaning clauses are generally unenforceable.
- Lease break fees: Not specified in statute — may be treated as unpaid rent or damages if provided in the lease, but not explicitly addressed in § 1950.5
- Unpaid utilities: Not specified in statute

## 7a. WEAR AND TEAR DEFINITION
- Statutory definition: Not explicitly defined in statute
- Court-established standard: Deterioration from ordinary use over time without negligence, carelessness, accident, or abuse
- Common examples courts have ruled as normal wear and tear:
  - Small nail holes from hanging pictures
  - Minor scuffs on walls from furniture placement
  - Carpet wear in high-traffic areas from normal use
  - Faded paint from sunlight exposure
  - Loose door handles from regular use
- Common examples courts have ruled as actual damage:
  - Large holes in walls
  - Stains on carpet from pets or spills
  - Broken fixtures or appliances from misuse
  - Burns on countertops or floors
  - Missing or broken blinds

---

## 8. PENALTIES FOR WRONGFUL WITHHOLDING
- Penalty multiplier: Up to 2x — § 1950.5(c)
- Multiplier trigger: Bad faith claim or retention of the security in violation of this section
- Statutory penalty: Up to twice the amount of the security deposit — § 1950.5(c)
- Attorney fees: Not specified in statute — § 1950.5 does not provide for attorney fees
- Burden of proof: Not specified in statute

## 8a. BAD FAITH DEFINITION
- Statutory definition: Not explicitly defined in statute
- Court-established indicators:
  - Complete silence — no return, no itemization, no communication after the 21-day deadline
  - Deductions taken without receipts or supporting documentation where required
  - Itemization sent after the 21-day deadline
  - Deductions for items that are clearly normal wear and tear
  - Landlord aware of violation and chose not to remedy
  - Pattern of similar complaints from other tenants

---

## 9. DEMAND LETTER
- Legally required before filing: No
- Strongly recommended: Yes
- Effect on bad faith argument: Significantly strengthens — continued silence after formal written demand is treated as strong evidence of bad faith by California courts
- Recommended waiting period after sending: Not specified in statute — typically 14 days or until the 21-day statutory deadline has clearly passed

---

## 10. FILING INFORMATION
- Court: Small Claims Court (Superior Court, Small Claims Division) — for claims up to $12,500 (individuals) or $6,250 (corporations/other entities); Limited Civil for higher amounts
- Maximum claim amount: $12,500 for individuals; $6,250 for corporations or other entities — Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 116.220
- Where to file: County where the defendant resides or where the rental property is located — Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 116.370
- Filing fee range: $0 – $150+ depending on claim amount and county — Cal. Gov. Code fee schedules
- Statute of limitations: 4 years (written lease) — Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 337; 2 years (oral lease) — Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 339
- Online filing available: Some counties — check local Superior Court website

---

## 11. SERVICE OF PROCESS
- Acceptable methods:
  - Certified mail: Yes — Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 415.30 (registered or certified mail, return receipt requested)
  - Constable or Sheriff: Yes — Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 415.10
  - Personal service: Yes — Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 415.10
  - Substituted service: Yes — on a competent member of the household or authorized person at the dwelling — Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 415.20
- Who can serve: Any person at least 18 years old who is not a party to the case — Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 414.10
- Proof of service required: Yes — Proof of Service form

---

## 12. LOCAL VARIATIONS
- Counties with known local rules: Vary by Superior Court — some counties require mediation or offer free mediation before a small claims hearing
- Cities with additional tenant protections:
  - San Francisco: Rent Ordinance requires interest on security deposits and specific handling procedures
  - Los Angeles: Rent Stabilization Ordinance (RSO) may impose additional requirements on deposits for covered units
  - Berkeley, Oakland, Santa Monica, West Hollywood: Local rent control and tenant protection ordinances may require interest or separate accounting
- Notes: Several California cities require interest on security deposits or separate accounting under local rent control ordinances. Agent must check local jurisdiction.

## 13. AGENT DECISION RULES

RULE 001:
  IF days_since_moveout > 21
  AND no_deposit_returned = true
  AND no_itemization_sent = true
  THEN violation_confirmed = true

RULE 002:
  IF violation_confirmed = true
  AND landlord_provided_no_communication = true
  THEN bad_faith_indicator = true

RULE 003:
  IF forwarding_address_not_provided = true
  THEN return_deadline_clock = not_paused_statutorily
  AND penalty_eligibility = not_reduced_by_address_alone
  AND agent MUST flag this to user as case risk
  AND note that landlord must send to tenant's last known address

RULE 004:
  IF damage_claimed_by_landlord = true
  THEN agent MUST evaluate:
    - Was damage documented on move-in report?
    - Does damage exceed normal wear and tear per section 7a?
    - Did landlord provide required receipts/invoices per section 6?

RULE 005:
  IF deposit_amount + penalties > 12500
  THEN agent MUST notify user of two options:
    - Option A: Waive amount above $12,500 and file in Small Claims Court
    - Option B: File in Limited Civil or Unlimited Civil Court — attorney strongly recommended

RULE 006:
  IF lease_required_notice_days IS NOT NULL
  AND notice_days_provided < lease_required_notice_days
  THEN agent MUST calculate:
    - Days short of required notice
    - Lost rent exposure to landlord
    - Whether this offsets deposit claim
  AND note that § 1950.5(a) allows landlord to delay return until end of notice period but still within 21 days after vacating

## 14. COMMON LANDLORD DEFENSES

DEFENSE 1: Tenant caused damage
  Landlord burden: Must provide itemized list with dollar amounts and copies of invoices/receipts (or description of work, time spent, and hourly rate if self-performed)
  Tenant response: Challenge each item against move-in report and wear and tear definitions in section 7a

DEFENSE 2: Tenant did not provide forwarding address
  Effect in California: Not a statutory defense to the 21-day deadline; landlord must send to last known address via first-class mail or personal delivery
  Tenant response: Provide any evidence of written address submission — email, text, certified letter; argue landlord had tenant's last known address

DEFENSE 3: We mailed the itemization on time
  Court standard: Mailing date/postmark controls; first-class mail to last known address is sufficient
  Tenant response: Request proof of mailing — if landlord cannot produce it, lack of timely itemization controls

DEFENSE 4: Cleaning clause in lease authorizes deduction
  Court standard: Non-refundable cleaning fees and blanket cleaning clauses are generally unenforceable; landlord may only deduct actual cleaning costs necessary to restore unit to initial cleanliness level per § 1950.5(b)(3)
  Tenant response: Argue clause is unenforceable; request receipts for actual cleaning costs
